FLying is A Privilege

if you don't believe me try flapping your arms.

So what is this all about then?

It is all about FLAP, a non-judgemental system/scheme for reducing global air travel to help save the planet.

Do we really need to reduce air travel? I've heard it only accounts for 2% of global emissions after all. Surely carbon-offsetting, biofuels and electric planes will save us?

Sadly not. Take a look at Figures 8.1 (p606) and 8.3 (p608) in ICC 2018 Report Chapter 8 to get a better idea of air travel's significant and rapidly increasing contribution to global CO2 emissions. This short article by United Kingdom Green Party MP Caroline Lucas explains why reduction is the only option. Finally this article, by George Monbiot, also short, explains why biofuels in particular are such a very bad idea.

Blimey I haven't got time to read all that stuff. Just tell me about FLAP so I can get on with my day.

Sure. It is pretty simple really - a scheme/on-line computer system for directly managing the total distance travelled by air each day. It works as follows:

  • Every time someone (a "Traveller") checks in for a flight the airline reports the details of the flight to FLAP. Airlines are legally obliged to do this.
  • FLAP maintains a "distance account", similar to a bank account, for every Traveller, with a balance of distance travelled by air.
  • Every time a flight check-in is reported FLAP debits the distance of that flight from the Traveller's balance.
  • A single value, the "Daily Total", specifies the total distance that can be travelled by air each day.
  • Each day FLAP credits all distance accounts in deficit with an equal share of the Daily Total.
  • If a Traveller attempts to start a new trip when in a negative balance FLAP instructs the airline to refuse the check-in. Airlines are legally obliged to do this.

Is that it? My <insert-single-digit-number-here> year old could have come up with that.

It was much more complicated to begin with. I kept removing bits until it didn't work any more and then reinstated the last bit I removed. In other words I put a lot of effort into getting it that simple.

Oh dear. I think you may have been wasting your time. You said FLAP is "non-judgemental". What do you mean by that?

I mean that it does not systematically judge or penalize frequent flyers. Rather it objectively and directly targets the core issue - reducing global air travel to a level that is environmentally sustainable. This manifests in three key ways:

  • No financial penalty or incentive is involved - a key benefit over frequent flyer tax schemes like A Free Ride
  • All travellers adhere to the same rules regardless of how often they travel.
  • Variation in the distance each person travelled is allowed, unlike in a traditional rationing scheme.

All I see is a few bullet points in a dull colour scheme. How can you be so sure how it would pan out in practice?

Because I "wasted" more of my time implementing it, implementing a global air traffic model to exercise it, and analysing the results. The system and model code are available on Github. Go to Modelling for a summary of the findings.

Hang on minute. I have to fly frequently for my work and family reasons and <insert-arbitrary-justification-here>. These results prove your scheme is unfairly targetting people like me.

I completely accept that the personal and professional situation of some mean they feel obliged to fly more often, and as discussed and shown, FLAP is pretty accomodating in this respect. However the bottom line is 70% of flights are taken by 15% of travellers, so air travel can only be signficiantly reduced through a change in behaviour of that 15%. This is not unfair targetting but objective fact. Another way of looking at this is that global air travel has tripled in the past twenty years. If you returned to the amount of flying you did in 2000, would that really be such a privation?

Well I "wasted" even more of my time writing this white paper considering these and many other aspects, which you are welcome to read, but specifically:

  • I know airlines can report all check-ins back to a central system because they are already doing it.
  • FLAP only refuses check-ins at the start of a "Trip", and its definition of what constitutes a Trip is extremely flexible. Someone would have to stretch this definition to its extreme limit to be at any risk of becoming stranded.
  • Yes there clearly needs to be more certainty for the traveller to make this a practical scheme. For this reason I am currently implementing new feature, based on the ideas discussed in 5.2 of the white paper, called "Clearance Promises", which will make FLAP much easier to use. See here.

My you have been busy. But really, you're focussing on the wrong thing here. Figure 8.1 clearly shows road travel is a far bigger problem that air travel.

They are both a problem, but yes you are right. We desperately need a practical plan for reducing road travel too. I believe that the principles guiding FLAP can be applied, but it is a bit trickier. If someone (anyone!) shows some interest I may have a go at coming up with something.
